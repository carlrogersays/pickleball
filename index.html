The Basics of Pickleball Singles

Court and Equipment

For a game of pickleball singles, you'll need a standard pickleball court, which is 20x44 feet in size. The court is divided into two halves by a net that stands at 36 inches tall in the center. You'll also need a pickleball paddle and a pickleball, which is made of plastic and has holes to reduce wind resistance. You can find pickleball courts near you by visiting websites like PickleballSpots or CanadaPickleball. Make sure your paddle meets the official specifications, which can be found on the PickleballEdmonton website. As for clothing and shoes, wear comfortable, athletic attire and shoes with non-marking soles.

Objective of the Game

For a game of pickleball singles, your objective is to hit the ball in such a way that your opponent cannot return it. Sounds simple, but it requires strategy, skill, and practice. You'll need to use a combination of serves, volleys, and groundstrokes to outmaneuver your opponent. Keep in mind, the key to winning is to control the pace of the game and force your opponent to make mistakes. Keep in mind that only the serving player can score a point, so you'll need to be strategic about when to take risks and when to play it safe. Court positioning is crucial in pickleball singles. You'll need to be able to cover the entire court, from the baseline to the kitchen (non-volley zone). Practice your footwork and court awareness to improve your chances of winning. Little do many players know, serving is a crucial aspect of pickleball singles. A well-executed serve can set the tone for the entire game and put pressure on your opponent. Underhand Serve Technique

Underhand serves are a staple in pickleball, and mastering this technique is necessary for success in singles play. To execute an underhand serve, stand behind the baseline with your feet shoulder-width apart, facing the net. Hold the paddle with your dominant hand, with your arm relaxed and your wrist firm. Make sure to keep your paddle below your waist, as this is a key aspect of an underhand serve. Toss the ball upwards, about 6-8 feet in the air, and strike it with the face of the paddle, using your wrist and forearm to generate power. Remember to keep your serve low and deep, aiming for the baseline to give your opponent less time to react. Practice your underhand serve regularly to develop muscle memory and consistency. Diagonal Crosscourt Serving Strategy

For a more effective serve, try using a diagonal crosscourt serving strategy. This involves serving the ball diagonally across the court, aiming for the opposite corner of the service zone. This type of serve can be particularly challenging for your opponent, as it requires them to move quickly to their right or left to return the serve. This strategy is especially useful in singles play, as it allows you to control the center of the court and create opportunities for winners. By serving diagonally, you're also making it harder for your opponent to return the serve with power, giving you an advantage in the rally. It's necessary to note that a diagonal crosscourt serve requires precise placement and timing. Make sure to aim for the correct spot on the opponent's side of the court, taking into account their strengths and weaknesses. You can practice this serve by watching videos and tutorials on CanadaPickleball or PickleballPretties, or by working with a coach or experienced player.

The Two-Bounce Rule Explained

If you're new to pickleball singles, understanding the Two-Bounce Rule is crucial to playing a successful game.

First Serve and Return Bounce

Rule number one: the first serve and return must bounce once before volleying. This means that when you serve, your opponent must let the ball bounce before hitting it back to you. Similarly, when you return the serve, you must let the ball bounce before volleying it back to your opponent. This rule is in place to prevent players from standing at the net and volleying the serve, giving them an unfair advantage. This rule also applies to the return of serve. If you hit a winner on the return, your opponent cannot volley it back immediately. They must let it bounce before hitting it back to you. Bear in mind, the Two-Bounce Rule is in effect until both players have hit a bouncing shot.

Volleying After the Two Bounce Rule

On the third shot and beyond, volleying is allowed. This means that once both players have hit a bouncing shot, you can start volleying at the net. Be careful, though - if you volley a shot that hasn't bounced, it's a fault and the point goes to your opponent. Volleying is a key part of pickleball singles strategy. By positioning yourself at the net, you can take away your opponent's angle and create opportunities for winners. Just remember to stay out of the kitchen (Non-Volley Zone) and avoid volleying shots that haven't bounced. For more tips on volleying, check out Pickleball Spots' guide to improving your volley. Return to the basics: if you're unsure about the Two-Bounce Rule, just remember that it's in place to prevent players from gaining an unfair advantage. By following this rule, you can ensure a fun and fair game of pickleball singles. And don't forget to practice your volleys - they're key to taking your game to the next level!

The Kitchen (Non-Volley Zone) Rules

Many players, especially beginners, get confused about the rules surrounding the Kitchen, also known as the Non-Volley Zone (NVZ). Understanding these rules is crucial to avoid faults and penalties during a game.

Defining the Kitchen Area

The Kitchen is a 7-foot area on each side of the net, extending from the net to the sidelines. This zone is marked on the court by lines, and it's necessary to recognize its boundaries to avoid stepping into it during a volley. You can think of the Kitchen as a "no-fly zone" for volleys, where you're not allowed to make contact with the ball in the air. Note, the Kitchen is not just limited to the area directly in front of the net. It extends to the sidelines, so be cautious when moving laterally during a rally.

Prohibited Volleying in the Kitchen

Kitchen rules are in place to prevent players from standing at the net and smashing the ball down into the opponent's court. This would make the game unfair and less enjoyable. When you're in the Kitchen, you're not allowed to volley the ball, meaning you can't hit it in the air. Kitchen volleys are considered a fault, and your opponent will score a point if you commit one. To avoid this, make sure to let the ball bounce before hitting it when you're in the Kitchen. You can still be aggressive and move forward, but be prepared to let the ball drop before making contact. Volleying in the Kitchen is a common mistake, especially when you're trying to poach or intercept a shot. However, it's necessary to resist the temptation and wait for the ball to bounce. Scoring in Pickleball Singles

For a thorough understanding of pickleball singles, it's important to grasp the scoring system. In this section, we'll examine into the specifics of scoring, game length, and server alternation.

Serving Player Scores Points

For every point won, only the serving player scores. This means that if you're serving and win a rally, you earn a point. However, if your opponent wins the rally, no points are awarded, and the serve is switched to the other player. Bear in mind, the serving player is the only one who can score points, so make the most of your serve! To maximize your scoring opportunities, focus on developing a strong serve and strategic gameplay. By doing so, you'll increase your chances of winning rallies and scoring points. Game Length and Winning Margin

On average, a game of pickleball singles is played to 11 points, with a two-point advantage (e.g., 11-9). This means that if you reach 11 points first, you win the game, but you must win by at least two points. If both players reach 10 points, play continues until one player leads by two points. Understanding the game length and winning margin is crucial, as it affects your strategy and gameplay. For instance, if you're leading 10-8, you may want to play more aggressively to close out the game, whereas if you're trailing, you may need to adopt a more defensive approach.

Server Alternation After Each Point

Length of the game aside, server alternation is another critical aspect of pickleball singles scoring. After each point, the server switches sides, which means you'll alternate serving from the right and left sides of the court. This rule ensures that both players have an equal opportunity to serve and score. Alternation of servers adds an extra layer of strategy to the game, as you'll need to adjust your serve and gameplay based on the side of the court you're serving from. Take advantage of this rule by practicing your serves from both sides and developing a strong overall game. Bear in mind, server alternation is key to a fair and exciting game of pickleball singles!
